My Story

I’m Jesse, a fourth year Data Science and Environmental Science undergraduate student at Griffith University.

The first computer I ever used was an old beige box PC running Windows 98. My early exposure to computers had me hooked from the very beginning. I was fascinated by them intensely. Throughout my childhood, through to teen-hood and now adulthood I have always been enthralled by the freedom and possibilities a computer provides.
When I was 12, I got an opportunity to visit the Cape Grim Baseline Air Pollution Station (CGBAPS) in Tasmania. This exposure to one of the most harsh and unforgiving landscapes imaginable immediately made me fall in love with the natural world. Still loving computers, I now returned home eager to experience more of the natural world.

During high school I excelled at maths and found myself enjoying it so much that I thought it would be a good career choice. I had considered math, physics, actuarial and engineering degrees but all failed to give my beloved exposure to nature. The end of school was only a few months away when I saw Griffith University had just brought out a new double degree – a bachelor’s in environmental science and data science. Instantly I knew this was the blend of nature and computation that I was seeking.
Shortly after finishing school and at the beginning of my tertiary studies I began employment at an aquaculture farm. Still working there in my final year of university, this job has taught me numerous life skills that coupled with my education give me a great understanding of marine environments, efficient workflow, technical skills, communication and more.

Having always looked fondly upon my visit to CGBAPS due to the influence it had on my life I decided to contact the head officer in my final year to do industry placement. To my delight they were happy to take me in. Now having completed my time at the station I can say it truly taught me so much more than I was expecting to learn about science as well as myself.
The work placement heavily revolved and solving various problems with Python. This included things like exploratory data analysis, the data science pipeline, microcontroller programming and classification/automation scripts. After working in the environmental realm for years this work placement was a fresh taste of what the data science aspect of my double degree has to offer.

Throughout these last few final months of my studies, I have been increasingly fascinated by machine learnings methods and artificial intelligence. Learning about and developing various machine learning models within my degree has shown me the massive potential this technology has to positively shape innovation in any field it is applied to. After my studies I aim to work in a data science (or similar) position and eagerly look forward to continuing my learning journey. My long-term career goals are rather malleable, but I always want to be working on something that gives back to the world and that I find great personal satisfaction with.

TLO 3 - Inquiry and Problem Solving


The threshold learning outcome (TLO) of inquiry and problem solving is imperative within the environmental management field as well as data science. This learning outcome is the ability to solve scientific problems by critically evaluating information from sources, designing/planning an investigation, selecting appropriate techniques, and drawing conclusions from recorded data.

The above process has many similarities to the policy cycle which I have learnt and put into practice within my environmental management major. Within my studies I have read many policies that tackle both climate change mitigation and adaptation. Some of these policies include the carbon tax, emissions reduction fund and various vague greenhouse gas emissions goals. These polices all follow the general structure of the Inquiry and Problem Solving TLO outcomes.

The policy cycle: Within these policies there is the problem definition phase whereby all relevant information is taken in from a variety of sources to determine if a problem is solvable by new policy. There is then the stage where the new policy is planned, after this a preferred and most efficient solution is chosen. After the policy is implemented, there is then ongoing evaluation to assess whether the policy is doing the desired job.

I have done this process within an environmental audit report of The Spit master plan as well as a policy analysis report on the QCoast2100 program by the Queensland Government.

TLO 4 - Communication


The ability to communicate clearly and efficiently is highly important in the realm of both environmental management and data science applications. Scientific knowledge and research is of reduced impact when important issues cannot be effectively communicated to all relevant persons. Studying a degree which was partly in person and partly online forced me to develop effective communication with the teaching staff and other students.

Throughout my studies I have worked on many reports which has helped me to practice scientific communication through a written media such as essays or reports. I have also written a lot of code during my time at university which has always contained clear communicative comments throughout. Within programming it is very important to have clear communication as the code always needs to be readable by a future user who may not understand the context or method in which it was originally written.

I have also worked in numerous group projects during my studies. These opportunities were fantastic in helping me develop my communication as groups had to be managed often with little or no in person interaction.

A specific application of my ability to communicate science is through the exploratory data analysis report I created for my WIL placement. This 20-page report included explanations of scientific methods used in the data analysis process as well as a summary and suggestions section. This communication was vital in enabling the CSIRO scientists to make an informed decision about the validity and path forward for the Aerodyne CO2 isotopologue measuring instrument.

My experience in effective communication is not limited to articulating quantitative data driven results but also more malleable things such as the interpretation of legislation. An example of this is my environmental audit report on the Spit master plan. This report contains clear and concise explanations of applicable legislation as well as having efficient methods to visualise the level of compliance the plan had with relevant legislation.

Work Integrated Learning Showcase


For my work integrated learning placement, I travelled to Tasmania to 24 days at the Bureau of Meteorology (BOM) Cape Grim/Kennaook Baseline Air Pollution Station (CGBAPS). The office is located in Smithton, Tasmania and the air monitoring station is a 50-minute drive west in Woolworth, Tasmania (the north-west point of the state). I was aware of the station due to having family living in Smithton. To get this opportunity I emailed the head officer Sam Cleland, and he was pleased to have me complete my WIL placement at the station.

The BOM is a government agency in Australia that is responsible for providing official weather services. On top of this the BOM also has several climate research stations around Australia. The Cape Grim Baseline air pollution station is one of three premier baseline air pollution stations in the world. BAPS are responsible for collecting air quality data that is considered ‘baseline’. This means the air has not been significantly contaminated by any major industries or cities.

During my time at the station I had three main projects. The first project was to perform an exploratory data analysis on a suspected faulty CO2 isotopologue instrument. My second major project was building a cloud classification script which classified and stored images of the sky at set intervals. This project was combined with a MAX-DOAS scattered light instrument to discover if any given spectra was taken whilst aimed at a cloudy or clear sky. My final major project was programming a Pi Pico microcontroller to automate calibration runs on a TEKRAN mercury analyser machine.

As well as these major projects I learned and helped out on many daily, monthly and annual tasks at the station. These included things like the 10m air intake service and clean, anemometer servicing and calibration, rainfall samples, various filter change procedures, the BOM automatic weather station and many more.

This experience taught me so much about independent efficient work, the climate change research process, python programming, problem solving and more.

I completed my three major projects within my 5 weeks at the station and at the end gave the officers a copy of my finished code repository. This finished code repository gave value to the industry partner as it provided solutions and answers to multiple underperforming or broken instruments at the station. Both my cloud classification script and microcontroller programs are applicable to stations across the globe who use the same technology. The officers at the station were very pleased with my work and assured me my microcontroller program and design would get put into use in other air quality stations – particularly a station in South Africa which had just purchased multiple TEKRAN mercury analysers.

These projects wouldn’t be possible without the many courses I have studied at university that taught me about climate change, data analysis and the python programming languages. Writing real-world applicable projects rather than practice exercises helps me truly learn about a process at a deeper level.
